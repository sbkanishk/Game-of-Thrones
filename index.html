House Targaryen

"Fire and blood" · sigil: the three-headed dragon · seat: Dragonstone

The dynasty that conquered Westeros with dragons three centuries before the story starts, then lost the throne when the "Mad King" Aerys was overthrown. This is why everyone keeps saying the throne is "rightfully" a Targaryen's.

Aerys II "the Mad King"

Last Targaryen on the throne
tap to expand
Grew increasingly cruel and paranoid as king. Overthrown by Robert Baratheon's rebellion after his son Rhaegar's actions and his own atrocities turned the realm against him.
↓ two children

Rhaegar Targaryen

Crown prince, eldest
tap to expand
Heir to the throne. His relationship with Lyanna Stark is the spark that sets off Robert's rebellion — and the throne's overthrow.

Daenerys Targaryen

Youngest, "mother of dragons"
tap to expand
Born in exile after the rebellion. Hatches three dragon eggs and builds an army to reclaim the throne her family lost. Aunt to Jon Snow, though neither knows it for most of the story.
Plus: Rhaegar and Lyanna Stark's secret son is Jon Snow — raised in the north believing he was Ned Stark's bastard. That makes Jon and Daenerys aunt and nephew, both with Targaryen blood, and Jon's claim to the throne technically outranks hers.

House Lannister

"Hear me roar" · sigil: the golden lion · seat: Casterly Rock

The richest house in Westeros. Tywin Lannister's three children each play the game completely differently — that contrast is the whole point of this family.

Tywin Lannister

Lord of Casterly Rock
tap to expand
Ruthless, calculating patriarch obsessed with the family's legacy. Effectively runs the kingdom through his children once his grandson Joffrey takes the throne.

Cersei Lannister

Eldest · queen, twin to Jaime

Married to King Robert Baratheon, but her children are secretly Jaime's, not Robert's. Ruthlessly protects her children and, later, the throne itself.

Jaime Lannister

Twin to Cersei
tap to expand
Sworn knight of the kingsguard, secretly Cersei's lover and the real father of her children. One of the show's biggest redemption arcs — for a while.

Tyrion Lannister

Youngest, "the imp"

Sharp-witted, often underestimated for his dwarfism. Despised by Tywin and Cersei. Becomes Daenerys's advisor and one of the most quoted characters in the show.
Cersei + Robert Baratheon's "children": Joffrey, Myrcella, and Tommen all sit on the throne or near it at various points — but none of them are biologically Robert's. They're Jaime's. This secret is the fuse that lights most of the show's political plotting.

House Baratheon

"Ours is the fury" · sigil: the crowned stag · seat: Storm's End

Three brothers, all with a claim, all wanting the throne for different reasons — this rivalry drives much of the early "war of the five kings."

Robert Baratheon

King, eldest brother

Led the rebellion that overthrew the Targaryens, married Cersei Lannister, and became king. By the time the story starts he's grown weary of ruling and dies in a "hunting accident" early in season 1.

Stannis Baratheon

Middle brother

Rigid, dutiful, convinced the throne is rightfully his after Robert's death since Joffrey isn't a true Baratheon. Pursues his claim relentlessly, at great personal cost.

Renly Baratheon

Youngest brother

Charming and well-liked, builds his own claim to the throne with House Tyrell's support, putting him in direct rivalry with his brother Stannis.

Vocabulary & titles

the old-fashioned words the show uses instead of plain english

A lot of the confusion in Game of Thrones isn't the plot — it's the language. These titles and terms come from real medieval history, so they sound ancient because they basically are. Tap any term for more context.

Titles & rank

Lord / Lady

ruler of land
The noble who governs a castle, town, or region under a king.
tap to expand
Ned Stark is "Lord of Winterfell" — he rules the north under the king, the way a regional governor answers to a head of state.

Hand of the King

chief advisor
The king's top minister — runs the government day to day.
tap to expand
Think "prime minister" or "chief of staff." Ned becomes Hand after Jon Arryn, the previous Hand, dies — which is what pulls the Starks into the capital's politics in season 1.

Ward

a hosted child
A young noble raised in another lord's household, often for political reasons.
tap to expand
Theon Greyjoy is Ned Stark's "ward" — officially a guest being raised at Winterfell, but really more of a hostage guaranteeing his family's loyalty.

Maester

scholar-healer
A trained advisor who handles medicine, science, and record-keeping for a noble house.
tap to expand
Part doctor, part librarian, part secretary. Every great castle has one — they wear a chain around their neck showing what they've studied.

Septon / Septa

priest / nun
Religious clergy of the main Westerosi faith, the "Faith of the Seven."
tap to expand
A septon leads worship and ceremonies; a septa often also tutors noble girls in religion and manners.

Master-at-arms

weapons trainer
The person who trains a household's young nobles and guards in combat.
tap to expand
Like a head coach for swordplay. This is who Arya and Bran train under at Winterfell before everything falls apart.
Family & legitimacy

Bastard

child born out of wedlock
A child whose parents weren't married — can't inherit titles or land under the law.
tap to expand
Bastards get region-specific surnames instead of their father's house name: "Snow" in the north, "Stone" in the Vale, "Sand" in Dorne, "Storm" in the stormlands, "Waters" near King's Landing. That's why Jon is "Jon Snow" — it's not a chosen name, it's a label marking his birth status.

Heir

next in line
The person legally entitled to inherit a title, castle, or throne.
tap to expand
Usually the eldest legitimate son. When Robb dies, the Stark inheritance gets messy precisely because the obvious heirs are dead, missing, or — in Jon's case — technically a bastard with no legal claim at all.

Betrothed

engaged to marry
Formally promised in marriage, often arranged for political reasons rather than love.
tap to expand
Sansa is betrothed to Joffrey purely to bind House Stark and House Lannister together — neither of them chose each other.

Kinslayer

killer of family
Someone who has killed a blood relative — considered one of the deepest taboos in Westeros.
tap to expand
It's treated as worse than ordinary murder. Several major character arcs hinge on whether someone will become — or already has become — a kinslayer.
War & oaths

Sworn sword

a loyal soldier
A fighter formally pledged to protect a particular lord or house.
tap to expand
Not a mercenary — a sworn sword has taken an oath of loyalty and is expected to keep it even when it's costly.

Bannerman

a vassal lord
A lesser lord who owes military support to a greater house.
tap to expand
When Robb Stark is crowned "King in the North," it's his bannermen — the smaller northern houses — who actually raise the troops for him.

Kingsguard

royal bodyguards
An elite order of seven knights sworn to protect the king for life.
tap to expand
Joining means giving up land, titles, and the right to marry — total devotion to the crown. Jaime Lannister is a Kingsguard knight for most of the story.

Night's Watch

the wall's guardians
A sworn brotherhood that guards the great wall in the far north against threats beyond it.
tap to expand
Recruits give up family, land, and titles for life — "I shall take no wife, hold no lands, father no children" is part of their vow. It's where Jon Snow goes to make his own way, since bastards inherit nothing at home.

Wildlings

free folk up north
People who live beyond the wall and don't answer to any southern king.
tap to expand
They call themselves "free folk" — "wildling" is the more dismissive name southerners use for them.
Address & honorifics

Ser

"sir"
The title given to a knighted man — earned through ceremony, not just born into.
tap to expand
It's the in-world spelling of "sir." Not every nobleman is a knight, and not every knight is high-born — it's a separate honor from a lord's title.

My lord / my lady

polite address
The standard respectful way to address nobility, regardless of how well you know them.
tap to expand
Used even between friends or family in formal settings — it signals respect for the title, not emotional distance.

Grace / your grace

how you address a king or queen
The formal address used when speaking directly to a reigning monarch.
tap to expand
Equivalent to "your majesty." Princes and princesses are usually addressed as "your highness" instead.

Khaleesi

Dothraki queen
The title for the wife of a "khal," the warlord-chief of a Dothraki horse-riding clan.
tap to expand
Daenerys earns this title through marriage to Khal Drogo — it's specific to Dothraki culture, not a Westerosi term.

most of these terms are drawn from real medieval English and French feudal vocabulary — the show didn't invent the strangeness, it borrowed it
